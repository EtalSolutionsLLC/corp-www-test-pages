Legacy systems are often described as technical debt. Sometimes they are. They may be difficult to maintain, expensive to integrate, or dependent on knowledge that lives with too few people.

But an older system may also be carrying business memory.

It may contain the exception process that keeps a difficult customer relationship working. It may encode the approval path that exists because of a regulatory lesson learned years ago. It may support a report that looks ordinary until the day someone discovers three departments quietly depend on it.

Modernization should not romanticize old technology. It should understand it.

Before replacing a legacy system, map what the system knows, what people have built around it, and which risks are hidden inside apparently simple workflows. The goal is not to preserve age. The goal is to preserve value while removing friction.
