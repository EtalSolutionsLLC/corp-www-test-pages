A.I. conversations often start with a tool. That is understandable. New platforms are easy to demonstrate, easy to compare, and easy to imagine as a shortcut.

The more durable starting point is the work itself.

Ask where people spend time assembling information that already exists. Ask which decisions are repetitive but still require judgment. Ask where delays come from searching, summarizing, routing, or reconciling information. Ask which tasks are frustrating because they are difficult, and which are frustrating because they are simply tedious.

Those distinctions matter. A.I. is useful when it improves a real workflow without hiding responsibility. It is less useful when it adds another layer of software to an unclear process.

Start with one contained problem. Define what good output looks like. Decide where a person must review the result. Measure whether the change saves time, improves consistency, or reduces avoidable effort. The tool comes after the work is understood.
