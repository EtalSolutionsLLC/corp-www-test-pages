The Skynet question is memorable because it gives A.I. risk a dramatic shape. The machine wakes up. The machine decides. The machine takes control.

The more immediate risk is less cinematic.

People may gradually surrender judgment because an automated answer is convenient, fast, and presented with confidence. Organizations may deploy tools before deciding who is accountable for errors. Teams may accept opaque recommendations because nobody wants to slow down an experiment that appears to be working.

The question is not whether a system has become self-aware. The question is whether people still understand where judgment lives.

Responsible A.I. use requires boundaries. Which decisions may be assisted? Which decisions must remain human? What evidence is retained? Who can challenge an output? What happens when the system is wrong in a way nobody anticipated?

The future is not determined by one dramatic moment. It is shaped by the small decisions people normalize along the way.
